Before we jump right into talking about data integrations, I think it is important to provide some context, and to give a brief overview of how a "typical" nonprofit website looks like in 2019 from high level.

Websites usually go through a few phases over time that might look something like this...

Most nonprofits treat their website as command central, and as their main public face to the world

Most of these websites are now running on some type of CMS

And most them are doing a good job covering the basics of messaging

(perhaps some of them are struggling with accessibility and responsiveness...)

Most sites also pretty quickly move on to Level Two, with Google Analytics and basic social media integrations

But at this level we are not looking true data integrations (despite what many RFPs seem to indicate!)

Instead this often means a very simple integration like Google Analytics, or things like iframe embeds, links off to other sites, and so on

Level three is where things start to get really interesting, and this is the kind of work that gets me really excited...

Most organizations have one or more very unique needs, or some type of custom data or application (and as we'll see in a bit, wilder's research library is a great example)

Typically these are tools or applications that are going to make things easier or better, or to provide some critical resource or tool to an audience.

I love creating solutions for things like this, but in the vast majority of cases, we are still working directly within the CMS, or building something "new" within it

LEVEL FOUR

To infinity...and beyond!

  • Third party integrations (e.g. SaaS)
  • Legacy integrations (e.g. homegrown apps)
  • Public data integrations (e.g. data.gov)
  • Any kind of integration!

And then we get to level four, which is really what the rest of this session will focus on

Here we get start talking about true data integrations, which typically involve an API or some type of custom importer where two systems are talking together and sharing data back and forth

The "data" in question can really come from anywhere, and often there are multiple sources

The vast majority of our client projects now include some of integration like this, and we have learned that every single one is a bit different
