My name is Tim Broeker, and I'm the technical director at Electric Citizen. Electric Citizen is a Minneapolis-based web agency, and we work almost exclusively in the civic sector (this includes everything from nonprofits and mission-driven organizations to higher ed and government work.)

A big part of my life involves this idea of data integrations, and helping organizations solve some pretty difficult problems that all surround this idea of data integration, and figuring out better ways of getting data from "here to there."

We recently worked with the Wilder Foundation on one of these integration projects, and we are fortunate to have Bryan Lloyd here with us from Wilder, who is going to help provide some perspective from their end of the equation as we explore some of these ideas, and some of the lessons we learned along the way. Before we jump right into talking about data integrations, I think it is important to provide some context, and to give a brief overview of how a "typical" nonprofit website looks like in 2019 from high level.

Websites usually have different levels of complexity, depending on your goals and your budget and your resources

The typical starting point, or what I'll call level one, looks something like this...

Most nonprofits treat their website as command central, and as their main public face to the world

Most of them are now running on some type of CMS like Drupal or Wordpress or some other system

Most them are doing a good job covering the basics

(perhaps some of them are struggling with accessibility and responsiveness...)

Most sites also pretty quickly move on to Level Two which I call psuedo integrations, with things like Google Analytics and basic social media integrations

But at this level we are not talking about real data integrations (despite what many RFPs seem to indicate!)

Instead this often means a very simple integration like Google Analytics, or things like iframe embeds, links off to other sites, and so on [COVER SLIDE]

If your stops at level two, you can probably get away with something like Squarespace or Wix or a simple Wordpress install

Level three is where things start to get really interesting, and this is the kind of work that gets me really excited and where Drupal really starts to shine

Most organizations have one or more unique needs, or some type of custom data or application (and as we'll see in a bit, wilder's research library is a great example)

Typically these are NEW tools or applications that are going to make things easier or better in some way, or to provide some critical resource or tool to an audience. [COVER SLIDE]

I love creating solutions for things like this, but in the vast majority of cases, we are still working directly within the CMS, or building something "new" within it...and often there is no real integration necessary

LEVEL FOUR

To infinity...and beyond!

  • Third party integrations (e.g. SaaS)
  • Legacy integrations (e.g. homegrown apps)
  • Public data integrations (e.g. data.gov)
  • Any kind of integration!

And then we get to level four, which is really what the rest of this session will focus on and where things start to get really interesting and complicated

Here we talking about TRUE data integrations, which typically involve an API, or some type of external data source where two systems are talking together [COVER SLIDE]

The "data" in question can really come from anywhere, and often there are multiple sources

The vast majority of our client projects now include some of integration like this, and we have learned that ALL of them are challenging, and every single one is a bit different
